✈️ Rubber-Band Powered Airplane (Propeller) — First Aerodynamics

Overview

Difficulty: Easy → Medium
Discipline: Aerospace (lift, drag, thrust, stability; energy conversion)
Core value learned: How a twisted rubber “motor” drives a propeller to generate thrust, and how angle of attack, dihedral, and CG set stability.

Background references:

  • Rubber-powered flight (context/history)
  • Rubber band powered airplane guide with images
Tip: Many hobby shops sell a balsa stick & tissue rubber-powered kit. This guide shows how to scratch-build a simple version if you don’t use a kit.

Tools & Materials

  • Propeller + rubber hook + rear bearing (buy an inexpensive “rubber power prop”/nose block set)
  • 1 long balsa stick (fuselage, ~6×6 mm)
  • Wing: thin sheet balsa (1–1.5 mm) or foam plate; span ~30–40 cm
  • Tail: horizontal + vertical stabilizers (thin balsa/foam)
  • Rubber motor: 1–2 loops of 3×1 mm rubber, ~25–35 cm long (start light)
  • Tissue or light tape (hinges), small clay for balancing, white glue/CA glue, pins, ruler
  • Optional: simple winder (or a pencil) to twist the motor

Before You Build — Feel the Physics

  • Energy flow: Twist → elastic potential energy → prop torque → prop thrust → airplane kinetic + potential energy.
  • Lift/drag & angle of attack (AoA): A small positive wing AoA makes lift; too much = huge drag + stall.
  • Static stability:
    • Dihedral (up-tilted wings) passively levels roll.
    • CG ahead of center of lift + tail downforce gives pitch stability.

Step-by-Step Build

  1. Fuselage: Cut a straight balsa stick ~30 cm. Glue the nose block/prop bearing at the front (ensure the prop spins freely).
  2. Tail boom & fin: At the rear end, glue a small vertical fin and a horizontal tailplane (e.g., 8 × 4 cm). Add a degree or two of tail incidence (tail slightly negative relative to wing).
  3. Wing blank: Cut a 35 × 8 cm rectangle. Dihedral: slice it at the center and reglue with each half tilted up ~5–8°.
  4. Mount wing: Rubber-band or tape the wing onto the fuselage ~40% from the nose (so you can slide it to trim).
  5. Rubber motor path: A thin wire hook at the rear of the fuselage holds the back end of the motor; front end loops onto the prop hook.
  6. Balance (critical): With prop removed, support plane on two fingers under the wing at ~25–33% of the wing chord. Add tiny clay to the nose until the plane sits slightly nose-down (CG ahead of lift).

Hand-Glide Tests (no turns yet)

Gently toss from shoulder height in a gym/field. It should glide straight for a few meters:

  • Stalls up then drops? Move wing forward (more nose-heavy) or reduce wing AoA.
  • Dives? Move wing back slightly or add a hair of up-elevator (tail trailing edge up).

Powered Tests

With 100–200 turns, launch gently level:

  • Turns too hard one way? Add a tiny rudder tweak (1–2 mm) opposite the turn, or slide wing a few mm.
  • Climbs then stalls: Reduce turns or reduce wing AoA/nose weight.
  • Won’t lift: Add a bit more thrust (more turns) or slightly increase wing AoA.

Why It Works (intuitive model)

The propeller converts motor torque to air momentum (thrust). The wing, at a small AoA, builds lift with minimal drag; dihedral centers the plane after a roll disturbance; a forward CG plus tail creates a restoring pitching moment. Trim moves (CG and control surfaces) change the balance of these restoring moments.

Safety

  • Props can bite. Keep fingers clear when wound. Don’t over-twist cheap rubber (it can snap).

Extensions

  • Try wash-in/wash-out (slight twist) to tame stalls.
  • Swap motor thickness/length to see burst vs duration trade-offs.
  • Build a lighter wing (sand to an airfoil) and aim for longer flights.
