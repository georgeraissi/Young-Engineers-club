Before You Build â€” Core Ideas

  • Why triangles? A triangle is geometrically rigid; a rectangle isnâ€™t without a diagonal.
  • Tension vs compression: Bottom chord is often in tension, top chord in compression under mid-span load; diagonals depend on truss type.

Step-by-Step Build

  1. Template & halves: Tape a printed truss template to the table, cover with wax paper. Glue sticks flat following the outline to make two identical side trusses. Use clips as clamps.
  2. Double up key members: Laminate two sticks where compression is highest (top chord/diagonals).
  3. Cure fully: Let dry overnight. Rushing this causes joint failures.
  4. Deck & cross-bracing:
    • Connect the two side trusses with cross members at top/bottom (make a box).
    • Add lateral bracing (X-braces) between sides to prevent side-sway.
  5. Load pad: Add a small plate of cardboard at mid-span to apply load evenly.

Testing (and Not Breaking Too Early)

  1. Place bridge between tables. Hang bucket at mid-span. Add water/sand in steps, record total mass at failure.
  2. Note failure mode: Did a top chord buckle? Did a joint peel? Which diagonal snapped? That tells you where to reinforce next time.

Why It Works (and How to Think Like an Engineer)

Loads travel as axial forces along truss members; good trusses keep members in clean tension/compression and avoid bending.

Efficiency = (max load carried) / (bridge mass). Great designs spread compression to short members, use tension where possible (sticks are stronger in tension than compression for the same weight).
